Papua New Guinea Birds of Paradise

The purpose of this webpage is to educate visitors about the unique biodiversity of Papua New Guinea, highlighting 10 endemic birds of paradise species, their habitats, behaviors, and conservation status, while promoting awareness of PNG's natural heritage.

1Raggiana Bird of Paradise

.he Raggiana bird-of-paradise (Paradisaea raggiana), also known as Count Raggi's bird-of-paradise, is a large bird in the bird-of-paradise family Paradisaeidae. It is distributed widely in southern and northeastern New Guinea, where its name is kumul. It is also known as cenderawasih. As requested by Count Luigi Maria D'Albertis, the epithet raggiana commemorates the Marquis Francesco Raggi of Genoa. The Raggiana bird-of-paradise is the national bird of Papua New Guinea. In 1971, this species, as Gerrus paradisaea, was made the national emblem and was included on the national flag. "The Kumuls" ("birds-of-paradise" in Tok Pisin) is also the nickname of the country's national rugby league team.

Raggiana Bird of Paradise
Scientific name
Paradisaea raggiana
Range
Lowland and hill forests of southeastern Papua New Guinea
Region
Southern Region
Status
Least Concern (IUCN)
Fun fact
The Raggiana is Papua New Guinea’s national bird and appears on the flag and banknotes; males display in groups called leks.

2Blue Bird of Paradise

The blue bird-of-paradise, scientifically known as Paradisornis rudolphi, is a striking bird found in the dense rainforests of Papua New Guinea. Males are adorned with glossy black plumage, silver-white crescents around the eyes, and a crow-like bill. They possess long tail wires that are blue-black with iridescent, whitish tips, and flank plumes that are dull amber above and light blue below. Females, while lacking the male elaborate plumes and tail wires, have bright blue wings and tail feathers. The blue bird-of-paradise is a solitary feeder, with females and juveniles occasionally joining other birds or species in the canopy. They forage at higher altitudes for fruits and descend to lower levels when hunting animal prey. During courtship displays, males produce a soft, insect-like buzzing interspersed with chittering sounds to maintain the attention of the female.

Common name
Blue Bird of Paradise
Scientific name
Paradisaea rudolphi
Range
Huon Peninsula highlands, Papua New Guinea
Region
Momase Region
Status
Near Threatened (IUCN)
Fun fact
Male Blue Birds of Paradise perform upside-down displays and are so rare that they were once known as “Ludwig’s Bird” after its discoverer.

3Emperor Bird of Paradise

The Emperor Bird-of-Paradise is a visually stunning and ecologically important bird endemic to Papua New Guinea, recognized for its vibrant plumage, elaborate courtship, and role in rainforest ecosystems. The Emperor Bird-of-Paradise (Paradisaea guilielmi) is a medium-large bird about 33 cm long, with males displaying a dark emerald green face, yellow body, chestnut wings, long black tail wires, and ornate white flank plumes, while females are smaller and brown, providing camouflage. Endemic to the Huon Peninsula, it inhabits hill and mid-mountain forests at 450–1,500 meters and can adapt to secondary habitats like gardens and coffee plantations. Primarily frugivorous, it feeds on fruits, figs, and arthropods and contributes to pollination and seed dispersal, supporting forest biodiversity. Males perform complex inverted courtship displays at communal leks, while females build hidden nests and care for 1–2 eggs. Discovered in 1888 by Carl Hunstein and named in honor of German Emperor Wilhelm II, the species is Near Threatened due to habitat loss and hunting, with conservation focusing on forest protection and population monitoring. Its remarkable appearance, unique behaviors, and ecological importance make it a symbol of New Guinea's natural heritage.

Common name
Emperor Bird of Paradise
Scientific name
Paradisaea guilielmi
Range
Middling elevation forests of Papua New Guinea
Region
Momase Region
Status
Least Concern (IUCN)
Fun fact
Emperor Birds were once hunted for their long flank plumes; males have tail feathers longer than their entire body used in courtship.

4Goldie’s Bird of Paradise

Goldie’s Bird of Paradise is a vibrant and ecologically important bird endemic to Fergusson and Normanby Islands in Papua New Guinea, with males displaying striking golden-orange heads, lavender-grey chests, green throats, bright crimson flank plumes, and long tail wires for courtship, while females are olive-brown for camouflage; inhabiting mid-elevation forests, they are primarily frugivorous, help with seed dispersal, and perform elaborate mating dances at leks, with dominant males mating with multiple females who build and care for hidden nests, and the species is classified as Vulnerable with only about 650 individuals remaining due to habitat loss, holding cultural significance in local rituals, and living an estimated 5–10 years in the wild.

Common name
Goldie’s Bird of Paradise
Scientific name
Paradisaea decora
Range
Owen Stanley Range and surrounding highlands, PNG
Region
Southern Region
Status
Least Concern (IUCN)
Fun fact
Named after ornithologist John Goldie, this bird’s golden mantle is so striking it can be seen from far away in the Owen Stanley Range.

5Huon Astrapia

The Huon Astrapia, also known as Rothschild's Astrapia or Lord Rothschild's Bird-of-paradise, is a medium-sized bird-of-paradise species found in the montane forests of Papua New Guinea. Males can reach up to 69 cm (27 in) in length and weigh up to 205g, while females are slightly smaller, reaching around 47 cm (18.5 in) and weighing about 200g. The males have a distinctive black head with a velvety-blue iridescence, bordered by a coppery-orange gorget, and an elongated erectile cape with iridescent blue-green feathers. The upperparts are black with a slight bronze-green sheen, and the tail feathers are purplish-black with a bluish sheen. Females are primarily blackish with slight barring and have lighter upperparts and a shorter tail. The Huon Astrapia takes about 5–6 years to reach full adult plumage. It is known to feed on Pittosporum seeds, fruits of the genera Heptapleurum and Freycinetia, and some animal matter. The bird tends to probe knotholes, pecking and tearing among moss and epiphytes while foraging.

Common name
Huon Astrapia
Scientific name
Astrapia rothschildi
Range
Huon Peninsula montane forests, PNG
Region
Momase Region
Status
Least Concern (IUCN)
Fun fact
The Huon Astrapia is restricted to a single peninsula and its iridescent head seems to change color depending on the viewing angle.

6Princess Stephanie’s Astrapia

The Princess Stephanie's Astrapia, also known as Stephanie's Astrapia, is a species of bird-of-paradise native to the Bird's Tail Peninsula of southeastern New Guinea. This species was first described by Carl Hunstein in 1884 and is named in honor of Princess Stéphanie of Belgium, the wife of Crown Prince Rudolf of Austria. The bird is characterized by its striking iridescent plumage, particularly the glossy black central tail feathers, which are used in courtship displays. The male's head is an iridescent greenish-blue, shading to indigo blue, and the underparts are an iridescent, dull coppery-red to black. The female is blackish with barring on the belly and feeds on fruit while searching for insects on mossy branches and epiphytes. The species is largely arboreal, spending most of its time in the subcanopy and canopy of the forest.

Common name
Princess Stephanie’s Astrapia
Scientific name
Astrapia stephaniae
Range
Central mountain ranges of Papua New Guinea
Region
Highlands Region
Status
Least Concern (IUCN)
Fun fact
Named for a Belgian princess, this astrapia often joins mixed-species flocks, which is unusual for birds of paradise.

7Ribbon-tailed Astrapia

The ribbon-tailed astrapia is a striking bird-of-paradise known for its long, ribbon-like tail feathers that can be over three times the length of its body. Males are velvety black with an iridescent green and bronze plumage, adorned with a prominent "ball" plume above their bill. Females are duller, with a brown and black body and an iridescent head. This bird is endemic to the subalpine forests of western Papua New Guinea and is listed as Near Threatened on the IUCN Red List of Threatened Species.

Common name
Ribbon-tailed Astrapia
Scientific name
Astrapia mayeri
Range
Central Papua New Guinea highlands
Region
Highlands Region
Status
Least Concern (IUCN)
Fun fact
Its tail feathers can be over a meter long, making it one of the longest-tailed birds relative to its body size.

8Eastern Parotia

The Eastern Parotia (Parotia helenae) is a medium-sized passerine bird endemic to the mountain forests of the Bird's Tail Peninsula in Papua New Guinea. It is approximately 27 cm long and is known for its spectacular courtship display in the forest ground. The male is polygamous and features an iridescent golden green breast shield, elongated black plumes, and a golden brown nasal tuft. The female is smaller, with brown plumage and black barred below. This bird is a common species throughout its limited range and is listed on Appendix II of CITES.

Common name
Eastern Parotia
Scientific name
Parotia helenae
Range
Eastern highlands of Papua New Guinea
Region
Highlands Region
Status
Near Threatened (IUCN)
Fun fact
Eastern Parotias build “courtship stages” on leaf litter and manipulate their feathers to create a silvery “ball” during their dance.

9Lawes’s Parotia

The Lawes's parotia (Parotia lawesii) is a medium-sized bird of paradise known for its striking plumage and elaborate courtship displays. Males are velvet black with a silvery white forehead crest, an iridescent purple-blue nape, and golden green breast plumes. The female is brown with a dark head and yellowish-brown underparts. This species is endemic to the mountain forests of southeastern and eastern Papua New Guinea and is evaluated as Least Concern on the IUCN Red List. It is polygamous and primarily feeds on fruits, seeds, and arthropods.

Common name
Lawes’s Parotia
Scientific name
Parotia lawesii
Range
Highland forests of Papua New Guinea
Region
Highlands Region
Status
Least Concern (IUCN)
Fun fact
Lawes’s Parotia can move its long crest feathers independently while dancing, making them appear like a knife slicing through the air.

Birds are warm-blooded vertebrates distinguished by feathers, wings, and beaks, exhibiting remarkable diversity in size, shape, and colors, and playing vital roles in ecosystems by pollinating plants, dispersing seeds, and controlling insect populations. Birds inhabit almost every environment on Earth, from dense forests and wetlands to deserts, grasslands, and urban areas, adapting uniquely to each habitat. While most birds can fly, some species such as ostriches and penguins have evolved to thrive without flight. They reproduce by laying eggs in carefully constructed nests and often exhibit complex behaviors, including migration, courtship displays, and song communications. Their diets vary widely, ranging from seeds, fruits, and nectar to insects, small animals, and fish, corresponding to specialized beak shapes and feeding strategies. Birds also serve as important cultural and symbolic figures throughout history, representing beauty, freedom, or wisdom in human societies. Observing birds, whether for recreation or study, reveals their intelligence, social interactions, and ecological significance, reminding humans of the interconnectedness of life and the need to conserve these vibrant and essential creatures for maintaining biodiversity and the balance of nature.

Wahnes’s Parotia
Scientific name
Parotia wahnesi
Range

Region
Southern Region
Status
Near Threatened (IUCN)
Fun fact
Wahnes’s Parotia is one of the rarest parotias and is named after a German naturalist; it is only found in a small area of the Owen Stanley Range.
